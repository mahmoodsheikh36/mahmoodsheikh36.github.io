1 true physics vector

a vector in physics may differ a bit from that of a math vector
a “true” physics vector is independant of the coordinate system we pick while a non-true physics vector may be dependant and its values may change when we change the coordinate system

2 position vector

the position of an arbitrary point P at \((x,y,z)\) is written as
\[ r = (x,y,z) = x\ihat + y\jhat + z\khat \]

the position vector isnt a true physics vector, because it doesnt really have a direction since it just represents a position in space
and so the position vector of a specific point in space may differ from one coordinate system to another, as we can pick any coordinate system we want and it wouldnt affect the position of a point

3 displacement vector

if we move from the point \(x_1,y_1,z_1\) to some new position \(x_2,y_2,z_2\) then the displacement defines a “true” vector \(S\) with coordinates
\[ S = (x_2-x_1,\ y_2-y_1,\ z_2-z_1) \]


a displacement vector is a true physics vector

4 instantaneous velocity

instantaneous velocity, usually called just velocity is a quantity that tells us how fast an object is moving at a single point in time and space
the SI unit for acceleration is metre per second (\(\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}}\))
velocity can also be defined as the derivative of position with respect to time, as it is the limit of the average velocity as the time interval approaches zero:
\[ v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t+\Delta t)-x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt} \]
using notation due to Gottfried Leibniz, who independently invented calculus. as Newton would have written
\[ v = \dot x \]
where the dot stands for \(\frac{d}{dt}\)

4.1 average velocity

average velocity is defined as the change in position or displacement \(\Delta x\) divided by the time intervals \(\Delta t\) in which the displacement occurs. the average velocity can be positive or negative depending upon the sign of the displacement. the SI unit of average velocity is meters per second (m/s).
\[ \bar v = \frac{x(t_2)-x(t_1)}{t_2-t_1} \]

4.2 speed

the concept of speed is sometimes useful. speed is simply the magnitude of the velocity: \(s = |v|\). in one dimension, speed and velocity are synonymous

5 acceleration

acceleration is the rate of change of the velocity of an object with respect to time
it can also be defined as the derivative of velocity with respect to time or the second derivative of position with respect to time
the SI unit for acceleration is metre per second squared (\(\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^2}\))

5.1 average acceleration

an object’s average acceleration over a period of time is its change in velocity, \(\Delta v\), divided by the duration of the period, \(\Delta t\):
\[ \bar a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]

5.2 instantaneous acceleration

instantaneous acceleration is a continuous function of time and gives the acceleration at a single point in time and space:
\[ a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v(t+\Delta t)-v(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2r}{dt^2} \]

6 position in polar form

although the directions of \(\rhat\) and \(\thetahat\) vary with position, the directions depend on \(\theta\) only, not on \(r\). As a reminder of this $θ$-dependence, we sometimes show it explicitly by writing \(\rhat(\theta)\) and \(\thetahat(\theta)\)

the position in a polar coordinate system is represented by
\[ \vec r = r\rhat(\theta) \]
using the chain rule we get
\[ \frac{d\vec r}{dt} = \frac{dr}{dt}\rhat(\theta) + r \frac{d\rhat(\theta)}{dt} \]

7 motion

suppose that the position of a particle is given by
\[ r = A(e^{\alpha t}\ihat + e^{-\alpha t}\jhat) \]
where \(A\) and \(\alpha\) are constants, find the velocity and sketch the trajectory

\begin{align*} v &= \frac{dr}{dt}\\ &= A(\alpha e^{\alpha t}\ihat - ae^{-\alpha t}\jhat)\\ v_x &= A\alpha e^{\alpha t}\\ v_y &= -A\alpha e^{-\alpha t} \end{align*}

the magnitude of \(v\) is

\begin{align*} |v| &= \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}\\ &= A\alpha\sqrt{e^{2\alpha t} + e^{-2\alpha t}} \end{align*}

to sketch the trajectory it is often helpful to look at limiting cases, at \(t=0\) we have

\begin{align*} r(0) &= A(\ihat+\jhat)\\ v(0) &= \alpha A(\ihat-\jhat) \end{align*}

note that \(v(0)\) is perpendicular to \(r(0)\)


as \(t\to\infty\), \(e^{\alpha t}\to\infty\) and \(e^{-\alpha t}\to0\), in this limit \(r\to Ae^{\alpha t}\ihat\), which is a vector along the \(x\) axis, and \(v\to\alpha Ae^{\alpha t}\ihat\); in this unrealistic example, the point rushes along the \(x\) axis and the speed increases without limit

10 gravity

according to Newton’s law of gravitation, two particles attract each other with a force that is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. gravity is always attractive.

consider two particles, \(a\) and \(b\), with masses \(M_a\) and \(M_b\), respectively, separated by distance \(d\), let \(F_{b,a}\) be the force exerted on particle \(b\) by particle \(a\), our verbal description of the magnitude of the gravitational force can be expressed mathematically as
\[ |F_{b,a}| = \frac{GM_aM_b}{d^2} \]
where \(G\) is the gravitational constant
the gravitational force between two particles is a central force because it is directed along the line joining them. vector notation is ideally suited for describing these properties mathematically. by convention, we introduce the vector \(r_{b,a}\) that extends from the particle exerting the force, particle \(a\) in this case, to the particle experiencing the force, particle \(b\). it is evident that \(r_{b,a}=-r_{a,b}\). note that \(|r_{b,a}|=d\), introducing the unit vector \(\rhat_{b,a}=r_{b,a}/d\), we have
\[ F_{b,a} = -\frac{GM_aM_b}{d^2}\rhat_{b,a} \]
the negative sign indicates that the force on particle \(b\) is directed toward particle \(a\), that is, the force is attractive. the force on \(a\) due to \(b\) is
\[ F_{a,b} = -\frac{GM_aM_b}{d^2}\rhat_{b,a} = +\frac{GM_aM_b}{d^2}\rhat_{a,b} = -F_{b,a} \]
where we have used \(\rhat_{b,a}=-\rhat_{a,b}\). thus the forces on the two particles are equal and opposite, as Newton’s third law requires

10.1 gravitational constant

the gravitational constant, denoted by \(G\), can be found by measuring the force between masses in a known geometry
the value of \(G\) for earth is \(6.673(10) \times 10^{-11}m^3kg^{-1}s^{-2}\)

10.2 sphere gravity

because force obeys the law of superposition, the force due to a collection of particles is the vector sum of the forces exerted by the particles individually. this allows us to mentally divide the body into a collection of small elements that can be treated as particles. we can then sum the forces from all the particles using standard methods from integral calculus
the force between a particle of mass \(m\) and a uniform thin spherical shell of mass \(M\) and radius R is

\begin{align*} F &= -G\frac{Mm}{r^2}\rhat & d > R\\ F &= 0 & d < R \end{align*}

where \(d\) is the distance from the center of the shell to the particle. if the particle lies outside the shell \(d > R\), the force is the same as if all the mass of the shell were concentrated at its center. if the particle lies inside, the force vanishes
