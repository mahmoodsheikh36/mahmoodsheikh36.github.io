1 true physics vector

a vector in physics may differ a bit from that of a math vector
a “true” physics vector is independant of the coordinate system we pick while a non-true physics vector may be dependant and its values may change when we change the coordinate system

2 position vector

the position of an arbitrary point P at \((x,y,z)\) is written as
\[ r = (x,y,z) = x\ihat + y\jhat + z\khat \]

the position vector isnt a true physics vector, because it doesnt really have a direction since it just represents a position in space
and so the position vector of a specific point in space may differ from one coordinate system to another, as we can pick any coordinate system we want and it wouldnt affect the position of a point

3 displacement vector

if we move from the point \(x_1,y_1,z_1\) to some new position \(x_2,y_2,z_2\) then the displacement defines a “true” vector \(S\) with coordinates
\[ S = (x_2-x_1,\ y_2-y_1,\ z_2-z_1) \]


a displacement vector is a true physics vector

4 instantaneous velocity

instantaneous velocity, usually called just velocity is a quantity that tells us how fast an object is moving at a single point in time and space
the SI unit for acceleration is metre per second (\(\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}}\))
velocity can also be defined as the derivative of position with respect to time, as it is the limit of the average velocity as the time interval approaches zero:
\[ v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t+\Delta t)-x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt} \]
using notation due to Gottfried Leibniz, who independently invented calculus. as Newton would have written
\[ v = \dot x \]
where the dot stands for \(\frac{d}{dt}\)

4.1 average velocity

average velocity is defined as the change in position or displacement \(\Delta x\) divided by the time intervals \(\Delta t\) in which the displacement occurs. the average velocity can be positive or negative depending upon the sign of the displacement. the SI unit of average velocity is meters per second (m/s).
\[ \bar v = \frac{x(t_2)-x(t_1)}{t_2-t_1} \]

4.2 speed

the concept of speed is sometimes useful. speed is simply the magnitude of the velocity: \(s = |v|\). in one dimension, speed and velocity are synonymous

5 acceleration

acceleration is the rate of change of the velocity of an object with respect to time
it can also be defined as the derivative of velocity with respect to time or the second derivative of position with respect to time
the SI unit for acceleration is metre per second squared (\(\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^2}\))


5.1 average acceleration

an object’s average acceleration over a period of time is its change in velocity, \(\Delta v\), divided by the duration of the period, \(\Delta t\):
\[ \bar a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]

5.2 instantaneous acceleration

instantaneous acceleration is a continuous function of time and gives the acceleration at a single point in time and space:
\[ a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v(t+\Delta t)-v(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \]
