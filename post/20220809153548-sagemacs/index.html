symacs, which stands for symbolic emacs is a latex-based interface to sympy built with emacs integration in mind
symacs looks for common math writing patterns and parses them into sympy objects to then evaluate them symbolically

2 sage vs sympy

sagemath is far sure much more powerful than sympy, but sympy is just a python library (about 10mb) and sagemath packs lots of software (including sympy and maxima) that amount to gigabytes of space, so i decided to just go with sympy, which hopefully will be much more powerful in the future as it has a huge community

3 parsing latex math expressions

texsoup does all of the work

from TexSoup import TexSoup
mytex = r'$E_1=\pmqty{1 & 0\\ 0 & 1},E_2=\pmqty{0 & 1\\ 1 & 0},E_3=\pmqty{1 & 0\\ 0 & -1},E_4=\pmqty{0 & 1\\ -1 & 0}$'
soup = TexSoup(mytex)
print(soup.expr.children[0].children)

3.1 parse matrix

from TexSoup import TexSoup
mytex = r"""
$\begin{pmatrix}
  1 & x & 3 & 4\\
  0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\
  2 & 1 & 3 & 0\\
  3 & 1 & 0 & -3
\end{pmatrix}$
"""

soup = TexSoup(mytex)
print(type(soup))
print(type(soup.expr))
print(soup.expr.children[0].children)
print(soup.expr.children[0].children[0])
env = soup.expr.children[0].children[0] 
m_str = [[expr.strip() for expr in entry.split('&')] for entry in env.all if not r'\\' in entry]
print(m_str)

from sympy import *
m = Matrix(m_str)
print('determinant:', m.det())
print('inverse:', m.inv())
print('tranpose:', m.transpose())

4 symacs limitations

texsoup fails to parse the following latex:

$\left(\begin{array} 1\\ 2\\ 3 \end{array}\right)$

as an array environment unless you add a space after \left(, so that it becomes:

$\left( \begin{array} 1\\ 2\\ 3 \end{array}\right)$
