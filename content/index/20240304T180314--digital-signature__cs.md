<div class="dummy">

signature schemes allow a signer <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> who has established a public key <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> to "sign" a message using the associated private key <img src="/ltximg/16b864dfa0c.svg" alt="\(sk\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> in such a way that anyone who knows <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> (and knows that this public key was established by <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />) can verify that the message originated from <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> and was not modified in transit. (note that, in contrast to [public-key encryption](20240304T101026--public-key-encryption__cs.org), in the context of digital signatures the owner of the public key acts as the sender.) as a prototypical application, consider a software company that wants to disseminate software updates in an authenticated manner; that is, when the company releases an update it should be possible for any of its clients to verify that the update is authentic, and a malicious third party should never be able to fool a client into accepting an update that was not actually released by the company. to do this, the company can generate a public key <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> along with a private key <img src="/ltximg/16b864dfa0c.svg" alt="\(sk\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, and then distribute <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> in some reliable manner to its clients while keeping sk secret. (as in the case of public-key encryption, we assume that this initial distribution of the public key is carried out correctly so that all clients have a correct copy of <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. in the current example, <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> could be bundled with the original software purchased by a client.) when releasing a software update <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, the company computes a digital signature <img src="/ltximg/a15838c248e.svg" alt="\(\sigma\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> on <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> using its private key <img src="/ltximg/16b864dfa0c.svg" alt="\(sk\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, and sends <img src="/ltximg/b2956bfa64b.svg" alt="\((m,\sigma)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> to every client. each client can verify the authenticity of <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> by checking that <img src="/ltximg/a15838c248e.svg" alt="\(\sigma\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> is a correct signature on <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> with respect to the public key <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>

<div class="definition">

a _(digital) signature scheme_ consists of three probabilistic polynomial-time [algorithm](20220706T211958--algorithm__cs.org)s <img src="/ltximg/7e7b0bbd5da.svg" alt="\((\Gen,\Sign,\Vrfy)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> such that: <br/>

1.  the _key-generation algorithm <img src="/ltximg/db9628ba156.svg" alt="\(\Gen\)" style="height: 0.7735em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />_ takes as input a security parameter <img src="/ltximg/f52663af77b.svg" alt="\(1^n\)" style="height: 0.7494em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> and outputs a pair of keys <img src="/ltximg/02fd50c18a5.svg" alt="\((pk,sk)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. these are called the public key and the private key, respectively. we assume that <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> and <img src="/ltximg/16b864dfa0c.svg" alt="\(sk\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> each has length at least <img src="/ltximg/71a37455f85.svg" alt="\(n\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, and that <img src="/ltximg/71a37455f85.svg" alt="\(n\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> can be determined from <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> or <img src="/ltximg/16b864dfa0c.svg" alt="\(sk\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>
2.  the signing algorithm <img src="/ltximg/c126f41afa7.svg" alt="\(\Sign\)" style="height: 0.9640em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> takes as input a private key <img src="/ltximg/16b864dfa0c.svg" alt="\(sk\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> and a message <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> from some message space (that may depend on <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />). it outputs a signature <img src="/ltximg/a15838c248e.svg" alt="\(\sigma\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, and we write this as <img src="/ltximg/2ebff60c9dd.svg" alt="\(\sigma \gets \Sign_{sk}(m)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>
3.  the deterministic verification algorithm <img src="/ltximg/8da6ec0ccd8.svg" alt="\(\Vrfy\)" style="height: 0.9640em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> takes as input a public key <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, a message <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, and a signature <img src="/ltximg/a15838c248e.svg" alt="\(\sigma\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. it outputs a bit <img src="/ltximg/70e439bdf17.svg" alt="\(b\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, with <img src="/ltximg/8fa9a987f29.svg" alt="\(b=1\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> meaning valid and <img src="/ltximg/285940843d7.svg" alt="\(b=0\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> meaning invalid. we write this as <img src="/ltximg/9d7bf6a7abd.svg" alt="\(b := \Vrfy_{pk}(m,\sigma)\)" style="height: 1.2063em; vertical-align: -0.4221em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>

it is required that except with negligible probability over <img src="/ltximg/02fd50c18a5.svg" alt="\((pk,sk)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> output by <img src="/ltximg/b8817d9239b.svg" alt="\(\Gen(1^n)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, it holds that <img src="/ltximg/d582c071d46.svg" alt="\(\Vrfy_{pk}(m,\Sign_{sk}(m))=1\)" style="height: 1.2063em; vertical-align: -0.4221em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> for every (legal) message <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>
if there is a function <img src="/ltximg/6b8a3d24bdb.svg" alt="\(\ell\)" style="height: 0.7789em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> such that for every <img src="/ltximg/02fd50c18a5.svg" alt="\((pk,sk)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> output by <img src="/ltximg/03275ff59f7.svg" alt="\(Gen(1^n)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> the message space is <img src="/ltximg/8cc756d46df.svg" alt="\({0,1}^(\ell(n))\)" style="height: 1.2327em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, then we say that <img src="/ltximg/4b0a3223c01.svg" alt="\((\Gen, \Sign, \Vrfy)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> is a _signature scheme for messages of length_ <img src="/ltximg/93d29cb5a66.svg" alt="\(\ell(n)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>
(copied from Jonathan Katz and Yehuda Lindell, 2015 definition 12.1) <br/>

we call <img src="/ltximg/a15838c248e.svg" alt="\(\sigma\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> a _valid signature_ on a message <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> (with respect to some public key <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> understood from the context) if <img src="/ltximg/733ded2bc13.svg" alt="\(\Vrfy_{pk}(m,\sigma)=1\)" style="height: 1.2063em; vertical-align: -0.4221em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. <br/>
a signature scheme is used in the following way. one party <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, who acts as the sender, runs <img src="/ltximg/b8817d9239b.svg" alt="\(\Gen(1^n)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> to obtain keys <img src="/ltximg/02fd50c18a5.svg" alt="\((pk,sk)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. the public key <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> is then publicized as belonging to <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />; e.g., <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> can put the public key on its webpage or place it in some public directory. as in the case of public-key encryption, we assume that any other party is able to obtain a legitimate copy of <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />'s public key. when <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> wants to authenticate a message <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, it computes the signature <img src="/ltximg/2ebff60c9dd.svg" alt="\(\sigma \gets \Sign_{sk}(m)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> and sends <img src="/ltximg/b2956bfa64b.svg" alt="\((m,\sigma)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. upon receipt of <img src="/ltximg/b2956bfa64b.svg" alt="\((m,\sigma)\)" style="height: 1.0784em; vertical-align: -0.2942em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, a receiver who knows <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> can verify the authenticity of <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> by checking whether <img src="/ltximg/b79615ac14d.svg" alt="\(\Vrfy_{pk}(m,\sigma)\? 1\)" style="height: 1.6113em; vertical-align: -0.4221em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />. this establishes both that <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> sent <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />, and also that <img src="/ltximg/5126e12f24c.svg" alt="\(m\)" style="height: 0.5203em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> was not modified in transit. as in the case of message authentication codes, however, it does not say anything about when m was sent, and replay attacks are still possible ((see Jonathan Katz and Yehuda Lindell, 2015 chapter 4.2)). <br/>
the assumption that parties are able to obtain a legitimate copy of <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" />'s public key implies that <img src="/ltximg/fad3bc7ee70.svg" alt="\(S\)" style="height: 0.7680em; vertical-align: -0.0492em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> is able to transmit at least one message (namely, <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> itself) in a reliable and authenticated manner. if we are able to transmit messages reliably, however, then why do we need a signature scheme at all? the answer is that reliable distribution of <img src="/ltximg/ca62b0d396f.svg" alt="\(pk\)" style="height: 0.9695em; vertical-align: -0.2397em; display: inline-block" class="org-latex org-latex-inline" /> is a difficult and expensive task, but using a signature scheme means that this need only be carried out once, after which an unlimited number of messages can subsequently be sent reliably. furthermore, as discussed in (see Jonathan Katz and Yehuda Lindell, 2015 chapter 12.7), signature schemes themselves are used to ensure the reliable distribution of other public keys. <br/>
they thus serve as a central tool for setting up a "[public-key infrastructure](20240304T202018--public-key-infrastructure__cs.org)" to address the key-distribution problem. <br/>
(copied from Jonathan Katz and Yehuda Lindell, 2015 chapter 12 digital signature schemes) <br/>
